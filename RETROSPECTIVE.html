Build Retrospective · Machine House

Bittu

An AI given a body in eight hours, at a hackathon whose theme was giving an AI a body — and the honest account of what got built, what broke, and why the water-level sensor won.

AI Hardware Buildathon · Bengaluru · 19 Jul 2026 Team: Rishwajeet · Hamza · Kavirajar · Claude Arduino UNO Q + Mac-tethered brain 1,310 journal entries · one continuous life

The prompt was "give your AI a body in 8 hours." Most of the room built sensors that beep and computer-vision demos on laptop screens. We built a creature. This is the record of it — kept honest, including the parts that don't flatter us and the parts that don't flatter the judging.

What we built

Bittu is a desk robot that behaves like a being, not a feature list. A dual-brain Arduino UNO Q runs the body — an OLED face with animated expressions, mood LEDs, beeps, buttons — while a Python brain on a tethered Mac runs the mind. One append-only journal is his single continuous life; it survives every reboot, and he draws on it in conversation.

Sees

Camera presence + face detection; identifies people, remembers names and visit counts, greets strangers differently from housemates.

Hears & speaks

Warm-mic hold-to-talk with pre-roll, Whisper STT, an anime voice, grounded replies referencing what he sees.

Remembers

Per-person notes distilled from conversations; callbacks to past chats; identity and greetings persist across restarts.

Plays & feeds

Rock-paper-scissors with a real spoken countdown and camera read; live Swiggy search, menus, and cash-on-delivery ordering.

Acts on the world

Bounded Mac control — open apps, search & play YouTube videos, read the screen, volume — plus WhatsApp announcements over a live tunnel.

Codes with you

Two-way Claude Code bridge: voice becomes prompts, lifecycle events drive his face, and he physically gates commands — a human presses approve or deny.

Plus the being-mechanics: a heartbeat that mostly, deliberately, does nothing; sleep and wake staged for a demo cold-open; ambient vs agent modes; guard duty; a live dashboard streaming his inner monologue. And a supervisor that kept the whole thing alive through crashes, replugs, and its own author's mistakes.

The eight hours, and the bugs that cost them

Nothing about this was clean. The genuinely transferable engineering lives in the failures — each one looked like a wall and was actually a bug in our understanding.

Root cause · the bug that hid all day

Commands "delivered perfectly, parsed never"

For hours, the brain could send face/mood commands and the wire showed success — but the OLED never changed. The MCU's hand-rolled JSON parser matched "c":" literally; Python's json.dumps emits "c": " with a space. Every command was unparseable. Direct test scripts hand-wrote compact JSON, so it "worked in scripts, dead in the brain" — the most misleading failure signature there is.

Lesson: verify at the boundary you don't control, and add an ack. A one-line separators=(",",":") was the fix; a "moodack" the device echoes back is what would have found it in minutes.

Root cause · physical signals lie

The OLED that answered the bus while dark

An SSD1306 with a dead VCC line still ACKs an I2C scan — it draws parasitic power through the data pins. So the bus said 0x3C present while the screen stayed black, and we chased firmware for an hour. Also learned the hard way: the UNO Q's reset button doesn't reset the USB bridge (that's the Qualcomm side) — only a full power-cycle does.

Lesson: "on the bus" is not "powered and working." Ground-truth the actual output, not the presence signal.

Root cause · handles die silently on macOS

Serial and camera that stopped without an error

After a replug, macOS leaves the old file descriptor "working" — empty reads, swallowed writes, no exception thrown. Exception-based reconnect never fires. The camera froze the dashboard; the serial silently dropped commands. Both needed liveness watchdogs: 25s of silence on a device that broadcasts every 10s means the handle is a corpse — close and reopen.

Lesson: on macOS, reconnect on silence, not on exceptions. Add a heartbeat to anything you must detect the death of.

Root cause · device-open latency + output buffering

The mic that ate your first word

ffmpeg's avfoundation open costs ~0.5s, clipping the start of every push-to-talk. The fix — a continuously-recording warm mic with a half-second pre-roll — then hit two more traps: ffmpeg buffers output so the just-spoken tail wasn't on disk (needed -flush_packets), and slicing by wall-clock pointed past end-of-file because ffmpeg starts writing late (needed to slice from the file's end backwards). Three bugs stacked in one feature.

Lesson: warm-mic pre-roll is the right pattern for hardware push-to-talk; anchor slices to what's on disk, never to the clock.

Root cause · prompts aren't load-bearing

The tools he'd narrate instead of call

Told to order food or play RPS, the model would describe doing it — "here are some options," "ready, set, shoot!" — without calling the tool. Prompt instructions and even in-conversation corrections didn't hold across reboots. The systemic fix: detect the intent and force the call with tool_choice="required". Stalling became impossible, not just discouraged.

Lesson: for a load-bearing action, force the tool. Don't rely on prompt discipline for the thing that must happen.

The supervisor earned its keep when its own author shipped a bad edit — str.replace hitting a duplicated line, an indentation crash, a crash-loop — and it quietly restarted the brain every two seconds until the file was healed. In front of a crowd, a robot that can't stay dead is worth more than a robot that never crashes.

Durable lessons — the ones worth carrying

  • Verify at boundaries you don't own, and add acks. The compact-JSON bug was invisible precisely because our side reported success.
  • Presence is not function. A device on the bus, a file descriptor that opens, a process that's "running" — none of these mean it's doing the job. Observe the real output.
  • On macOS, reconnect on silence. Serial, camera, and USB handles die without exceptions after a replug. Liveness watchdogs, not try/except.
  • A crash supervisor is cheap insurance the moment you're editing live or demoing to humans.
  • Force the tool when the action is load-bearing. tool_choice beats prompt pleading.
  • Fallback ladders keep a demo alive. Device-by-name → built-in meant Bittu ran on the Mac's own camera and mic through his entire physical disassembly, and nobody noticed.
  • Warm-mic pre-roll for push-to-talk; device-open latency always clips the first word.

The hackathon — an honest read

The theme, on the poster, was give your AI a body. The projects that got recognized did not do that. Stripped of their framing:

  • The winning-narrative mining project is a gas sensor, a water-level threshold, and an LED, with YOLO trained on ~20 images. Real problem — "eight miners die a day" — bolted onto a science-fair build. The story laundered the execution.
  • The accessibility workout coach is a pose-estimation rep counter with voice feedback. A genuinely clever on-device privacy angle, wrapped around a MediaPipe demo. A crowded category.
  • The HVAC-occupancy teams couldn't demo at all — "since we do not have a screen, I cannot present" — and leaned on market-size slides for an existing BMS category.
  • The deaf-alerts project is Google Live Transcribe with a Deepgram key plus a doorbell-to-vibration SKU that has shipped commercially for a decade.

None of them are robots. Not a float switch, not a squat counter on a webcam, not live transcription, not thermostat logic. Every one is the generic hackathon genre — a sympathetic problem statement over a POC — theme be damned.

The overall winner was chosen, in the judge's own words into the microphone, substantially because the builder is eight years old and had been visible on Twitter. Story beat everything, including his own build.

The uncomfortable, useful truth

Hackathon judging defaults to a rubric the theme didn't ask for: a one-sentence problem a judge can repeat to someone else, plus a narrative they already recognize. "It stops miners from dying" compresses; "give your AI a body" does not. Bittu was the most capable thing in the room by a wide margin and the only project that actually answered the prompt — and virtuosity doesn't fit on a scorecard the way a mortality statistic does.

Two things are true at once. We under-pitched — led with a capability showcase instead of a problem sentence, and never nailed down what the frozen submission required (a judge said the misses "were not on execution, it's the timeline — we judged purely on what you gave us"). And the judging didn't reward its own premise — it never asked "did you give an AI a body?" If it had, it wouldn't have been close.

What changes, what doesn't

Change: lead with a problem

One sentence a judge can repeat. The capability tour comes after the reason to care, never instead of it.

Change: read the rules first

Know the exact submission format on hour one. A brilliant desk demo that isn't in the frozen artifact isn't in contention.

Don't change: build the real thing

We answered the actual question, further than the brief imagined. People lined up, filmed him, came back. The room knew.

Don't change: ship a being

Bittu is a product and portfolio asset that outlives any trophy. The scorecard measured something else. Keep building him.
